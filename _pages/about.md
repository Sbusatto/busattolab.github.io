main_heading: Decoding Extracellular Vesicles to Defeat Metastasis
main_description: |
  Welcome to the **Biological Nanoparticles Laboratory**. We are an independent research group within the **Nanomedicine and Drug Delivery Department** at the Groningen Research Institute of Pharmacy (University of Groningen). Led by **Dr. Sara Busatto**—following her tenure at **Harvard Medical School** and **Boston Children’s Hospital**—our lab focuses on the intersection of biological nanoparticles and cancer progression. We investigate the critical role of **Extracellular Vesicles (EVs)** in driving **Breast-to-Brain Metastasis** and leverage these insights to engineer targeted, next-generation **EV-mimetic therapeutics**.
pillars:
  - title: Breast to Brain Metastasis
    description: |
      **Investigating the role of cancer-derived EVs in the early steps of metastasis.**

      Our laboratory investigates the role of cancer-derived extracellular vesicles (EVs) in the very early steps that lead to the formation of breast to brain metastasis.

      In particular, we are focused on how the preparation of a brain microenvironment—better known as the pre-metastatic niche—is influenced by EVs to favor the survival and growth of metastatic cells.

  - title: EV-Mimetics & Drug Delivery
    description: |
      **Loading and engineering EV-mimetics as biocompatible targeted vehicles.**

      We are developing novel and scalable engineering methods applicable to EVs and EV-mimetics to create actively targeted drug delivery vehicles.

      Our proprietary methods allow for the scalable and rapid synthesis of cell-membrane-derived EV-mimicking nanoparticles. We aim to utilize these vehicles to deliver small non-coding genetic material, proteins, and standard chemotherapies directly to primary and metastatic breast cancer cells.

  - title: The Biomolecular Corona
    description: |
      **Studying the formation and composition of the biomolecular corona of cancer-derived EVs.**

      Nanoparticles introduced into biological fluids rapidly acquire a coating of biomolecules, known as the biomolecular corona.

      Our laboratory studies the formation, composition, and functional impact of this biomolecular corona on cancer-derived EVs. Understanding these interactions is critical for elucidating the physiological role of EVs and for optimizing them as therapeutic delivery systems.
